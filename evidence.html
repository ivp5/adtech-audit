The Accountability Gap

Programmatic advertising supply chain fraud, consent failure, and identity proliferation.
Evidence from 112,000 websites, 24 SSP registries, 273,000 identity-sharing requests.
Data collected March 14–18, 2026.

Finding 1: Authorization Is Forged

Core claim

74% of "DIRECT" authorization claims in publisher ads.txt files are provably false. Mechanical cross-reference against 24 SSP seller registries. No interpretation required.

When a publisher lists a company as "DIRECT" in their ads.txt file, it means: "I control this seller account. I authorized this directly." Advertisers pay more for DIRECT inventory because it's supposed to mean fewer intermediaries and less fraud risk.

We checked 177,897 unique (publisher, SSP, seller_id) triples against the SSPs' own registries:

CategoryCountDistributionHow verified
Contradicted66,901 (37%)
SSP says INTERMEDIARY, not PUBLISHER
Phantom66,109 (37%)
Seller ID doesn't exist in registry
Plausible44,887 (25%)
Registry confirms PUBLISHER type

Of the 25% marked "plausible," approximately 33,000 are shared by 5+ publishers claiming the same PUBLISHER seller ID — mathematically, at most one can be the actual direct controller. This narrows the genuinely plausible range to ~6%, giving a 74–95% false range.

Why it happens

Intermediaries distribute template ads.txt blocks to publishers with DIRECT instead of RESELLER. DIRECT commands higher CPMs and lower SSP fees (Magnite charges intermediaries 1.3 percentage points more than publishers). The intermediary's margin becomes invisible to the buyer.

Finding 2: Consent Is Absent

Core claim

0.012% of identity-sharing requests carry valid consent on first visit. 34 genuine consent strings in 272,917 sync requests. Not broken — never built.

Under GDPR, companies must obtain consent before sharing a user's identity with third parties. In programmatic advertising, "cookie syncing" is the mechanism that shares identity — Company A tells Company B "your user #X is my user #Y."

We captured 272,917 cookie sync requests across 110,000 websites and checked whether they carried the required TCF consent string:

Consent StatusRequestsDistribution
No consent parameter at all210,980 (77.3%)
Parameter present but empty60,109 (22.0%)
Unresolved template macro1,794 (0.66%)
Valid TCF consent string34 (0.012%)

What the user experiences

0.0 seconds — Page load begins

Ad scripts start executing. Cookie sync requests fire immediately.

0.1–0.5 seconds — Identity propagation

User IDs shared across companies. No consent obtained yet.

1–3 seconds — Full graph assembled

232 companies now know this user visited this page. Graph has 3 waves of propagation.

2–5 seconds — Consent banner appears

User sees "Do you accept cookies?" The data has already been shared.

0 of 2,000 captured Prebid.js instances configure the consent management module. The Prebid Mobile SDK (decompiled from production binary) defaults to "full device access" when no consent management platform is present — including broadcasting the device's permanent Advertising ID to all bidders.

Known Weaknesses

1. Sample bias. 4,411 publishers from Tranco top-1M. Biased toward popular Western commercial sites. This is the relevant population for programmatic authorization, but not the internet.

2. sellers.json freshness. Snapshots from March 17, 2026. Contradicted claims (37%) can't change retrospectively — a registry saying INTERMEDIARY today means INTERMEDIARY. Phantom claims could resolve if SSPs update registries, but contradicted alone is 37%.

3. First-visit consent. Returning users with consent cookies likely show higher consent rates. The first-visit case is the privacy-critical one — data flows before consent is possible — but should not be generalized to all visits.

4. Quick crawl undercounting. 4-second page loads. Deep scans (12+ seconds) see 2.4x more tracking entities. The true median for fully-loaded pages is likely 5–7, not 3.

5. Sharing analysis range. The 74–95% range reflects genuine ambiguity. Multi-domain publishers (Learfield, Bonnier, Postmedia) have legitimate shared DIRECT claims. The floor is 74%. The ceiling depends on a judgment call about how many publishers can share one account.

Why It Persists

Every validation check that could fix these findings would cost money to the party responsible for implementing it:

If this check workedWho loses revenueHow much
SSPs validate seller_ids against their own registrySSPs (lose billable inventory)~37% of DIRECT slots
DSPs verify DIRECT claims against sellers.jsonDSPs (campaigns underdeliver)~74% of "premium" supply
Servers reject syncs without valid consentEveryone (identity graph collapses)~99.99% of sync traffic
Publishers audit their own ads.txtPublishers (fewer bidders, lower CPMs)Est. 10-30% CPM drop

Every participant profits from every check not working. No single actor can start validating without losing money while competitors who don't validate gain share. The system is stable because it's broken.

The accountability standards (ads.txt, TCF, sellers.json) were written by the IAB — a trade association whose members benefit from exactly the failures documented above. This is not fraud, negligence, or regulatory failure. It is a Nash equilibrium: a stable state where no participant can improve their position by changing strategy alone.

Breaking the equilibrium requires either coordinated enforcement (penalties exceeding the revenue from non-compliance) or giving the extracted parties — advertisers paying for unverified inventory and users tracked without consent — the audit tools to see through the intermediary layer. This dataset is the second option.
