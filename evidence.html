The Accountability Gap

Programmatic advertising supply chain fraud, consent failure, and identity proliferation.
Evidence from 125,000 websites, 63 SSP registries, 273,000 identity-sharing requests.
Data collected March 14–18, 2026.

Finding 1: Authorization Is Forged

Core claim

53% of "DIRECT" authorization claims are explicitly contradicted by the SSPs' own registries. A further 35% reference seller IDs that don't exist. Cross-reference of 657,286 triples against 63 SSP seller registries. The strict rate (contradicted only) is 53%. The inclusive rate (contradicted + phantom) is 70%.

When a publisher lists a company as "DIRECT" in their ads.txt file, it means: "I control this seller account. I authorized this directly." Advertisers pay more for DIRECT inventory because it's supposed to mean fewer intermediaries and less fraud risk.

We checked 657,286 unique (publisher, SSP, seller_id) triples against the SSPs' own registries:

Reading this table: "Contradicted" is unambiguous — the SSP explicitly classifies the account as INTERMEDIARY, contradicting the DIRECT claim. "Phantom" is ambiguous — the seller ID may be stale, fabricated, or hidden behind Google's 71% confidentiality flag. The strict false rate (contradicted only) is 53%. The inclusive rate (contradicted + phantom) is 70%. Both rates are stable across 5 successive SSP expansions (14→24→37→62→63 SSPs).

Why it happens

Intermediaries (AnyManager, PubFuture, Waardex, YieldMonk) distribute template ads.txt blocks to publishers with DIRECT instead of RESELLER. DIRECT commands higher CPMs and lower SSP fees. The intermediary's margin becomes invisible to the buyer.

Finding 2: Consent Is Absent

Core claim

0.012% of identity-sharing requests carry valid consent on first visit. 34 genuine consent strings in 272,917 sync requests. Not broken — never built.

Under GDPR, companies must obtain consent before sharing a user's identity with third parties. In programmatic advertising, "cookie syncing" is the mechanism that shares identity — Company A tells Company B "your user #X is my user #Y."

We captured 272,917 cookie sync requests across 110,000 websites and checked whether they carried the required TCF consent string:

Consent StatusRequestsDistribution
No consent parameter at all210,980 (77.3%)
Parameter present but empty60,109 (22.0%)

Valid TCF consent string34 (0.012%)

What the user experiences

0.0 seconds — Page load begins

Ad scripts start executing. Cookie sync requests fire immediately.

0.1–0.5 seconds — Identity propagation

User IDs shared across companies. No consent obtained yet.

1–3 seconds — Full graph assembled

232 companies now know this user visited this page. Graph has 3 waves of propagation.

2–5 seconds — Consent banner appears

User sees "Do you accept cookies?" The data has already been shared.

0 of 2,000 captured Prebid.js instances configure the consent management module. The Prebid Mobile SDK (decompiled from production binary) defaults to "full device access" when no consent management platform is present — including broadcasting the device's permanent Advertising ID to all bidders.

Known Weaknesses

1. Sample bias. 9,415 publishers from Tranco top-1M + crawler harvest. Biased toward popular Western commercial sites. This is the relevant population for programmatic authorization, but not the internet.

2. sellers.json freshness. Snapshots from March 17–18, 2026. SSPs can reclassify sellers — an INTERMEDIARY today could become PUBLISHER tomorrow. Phantom claims could resolve if SSPs add entries. Both verdicts are point-in-time. The strict contradicted rate (35%) is the most defensible number; it requires the SSP to have actively classified the seller as INTERMEDIARY at snapshot time.

3. First-visit consent. Returning users with consent cookies likely show higher consent rates. The first-visit case is the privacy-critical one — data flows before consent is possible — but should not be generalized to all visits.

4. Quick crawl undercounting. 4-second page loads. Deep scans (12+ seconds) see 2.4x more tracking entities. The true median for fully-loaded pages is likely 5–7, not 3.

5. Two rates, one dataset. The strict rate (53%) counts only claims where the SSP explicitly says INTERMEDIARY. The inclusive rate (70%) also counts phantom seller IDs. Both are stable across expansions from 14 to 63 SSPs. The strict rate is the floor no one can argue with. The inclusive rate is the ceiling that depends on whether phantom IDs are fabricated or merely stale. Google's 71% confidentiality flag makes their phantom claims genuinely ambiguous.
